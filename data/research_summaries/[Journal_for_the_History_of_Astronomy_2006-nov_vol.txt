A LETTER FROM WILLIAM GASCOIGNE
TO SIR KENELM DIGBY
DAVID SELLERS, Leeds, U.K.
The advent of a new transit of Venus season has brought once more into focus the
remarkable yet brief lives of three young astronomers who blazed a trail for precision
astronomy in England. The contribution of Jeremiah Horrocks (c. 1619-41) scarcely
needs any introduction: he embraced Keplerian astronomy before most of his peers,
produced an improved theory of the Moon's motion, and earned enduring fame for
his prediction of the 1639 transit of Venus and his meticulous observation of it. His
friend William Crabtree (1610-44), a draper of Broughton, near Manchester, alerted
to the prospect of the event by Horrocks, also observed the transit and bore witness
to the tiny size of the Venusian disc on the face of the Sun. Crabtree was an accom­
plished observer and was skilful in the mathematical aspects of astronomy. Much of
our knowledge of Horrocks and his work arises from the extensive correspondence
that he and Crabtree maintained.
Of the third young astronomer, William Gascoigne (c.1612-e.I644), far less is
known. Crabtree had made Gascoigne's acquaintance around 1639-40 and in 1640
visited his home at Middleton, near Leeds inYorkshire. Through this relationship, both
Crabtree and Horrocks had become aware of the ingenious inventions of the Leeds
astronomer in the field oftelescopic optics. Between the years 1640and 1642Crabtree
and Gascoigne exchanged a substantial series of letters. Following the death of their
authors, these letters came into the possession of Richard Towneley (1629-1707).
In 1672 John Flamsteed, "by the singular favour of that ingenuous & right worthy
Gentleman Richard Townly of Townly", I spent some time at Towneley Hall, near
Burnley, transcribing the correspondence. Shortly afterwards, he drafted a brieftrea­
tise discussing its astronomical and optical content. This survives in the Flamsteedl
RGO papers.' Unfortunately, the treatise, which was never published, contains only
selected excerpts from the correspondence, since, in Flamsteed's words, the whole
was "too prolix to transcribe altogeather, & conteineing some things immateriall to our
purpose".' In 1712 the Gascoigne-Crabtree correspondence passed into the hands of
the Rev.William Derham (1657-1735) through the generosity of Richard Towneley' s
son, Charles (1658-1712). In the same year, Derham published one of the letters (from
Crabtree to Gascoigne) in the Philosophical transactions of the Royal Society" and,
five years later,prompted by a French claim to have invented the telescope micrometer,
he published further fragments.' The originals sadly seem to have disappeared. Until
recently, it had been thought that the only letters from Gascoigne to have survived
were the two that he wrote to the mathematician, William Oughtred (1573?-1660) in
1640-41. These letters remained hidden in the private Macclesfield Collection until
they were purchased by the University of Cambridge Library in 2000. 6 Transcriptions

It is perhaps partly due to the paucity of primary source material relating to
William Gascoigne - including correspondence - that he is so neglected today.
Neither in his home town, nor anywhere in the U.K., is there any public memorial
to his accomplishments.
Gascoigne invented the telescopic sight and the telescope micrometer. He made
seminal observations of the Moon, to which Flamsteed dedicated the first few pages
of his monumental Historiae coelestis Britannicae. His expertise in the analysis of
optical systems was such that Flamsteed was prompted to declare that
Amongst Mr. Gascoigne's letters I found some wherein he showed how the images
of remote objects were formed in the distinct base of a convex object glass. From
these I got my dioptrics in a few hours; having read Descartes' Dioptrics before,
but learnt little by them because he discourses not of this subject."
William Molyneux, in his Dioptrica nova, admits to being similarly indebted to
Gascoigne's originality when he says in the Preface, or Admonition to the Reader,
I can say that the Geometrical method of Calculating a Rays Progress, which
in many particulars is so amply delivered hereafter, is wholly new, and never
before publish'd. And for the first Intimation thereof, I must acknowledg my
self obliged to my worthy Friend Mr. Flamsteed, Astron. Reg., who had it from
some unpublished Papers of Mr. Gascoignes.'?
The optical instruments that Gascoigne invented were seemingly lost without trace
following his untimely death in the English Civil War. Their like was not seen again
until the micrometer was re-invented, apparently independently, by Adrien Auzout
and others in the I 660s. Perhaps Gascoigne's most effective legacy was his contribu­
tion to the optical instruction of those who, a generation or so later, would dedicate
their lives to the meticulous measurement of the heavens. It is a pleasant surprise to
find that, lurking in the manuscript archives of the British Library, now searchable
via the Internet, is a hitherto uncited letter of 30 November 16[40] from Gascoigne
to Sir Kenelm Digby I I and a lengthier one from William Crabtree to Gascoigne."
Thus the number of surviving letters in Gascoigne's own hand is increased by 50%
and a little more light is shed on their author. The letter to Digby, which is slightly
damaged and illegible in places, is transcribed in the Appendix to this paper.
We already knew that Gascoigne had met Digby and had corresponded with him.
As the following extract of his second letter to Oughtred makes clear, the young
astronomer, previously pursuing his studies in a solitary fashion, had started to make the
acquaintance of a number of persons who were showing a keen interest in his work.

industrious astronomers, who are not a little taken with them, and the more,
because they believed they had tried all possible means, that glasses could afford

Crabtree was unstinting in his admiration:

The identity of Gascoigne's "noble friend", who prematurely divulged details of his
invention, is not made clear and must remain a matter for speculation and hypothesis.
The peer of the realm in question would surely have to have been enthused by the
micrometer in just the same way as Crabtree in order to have "bolted out" the details.
There cannot have been many such peers in an age when most preferred hunting and
hounds as leisure pursuits, rather than science. Perhaps the anonymous individual
was William Cavendish, Marquess, and ultimately Duke, of Newcastle (1592-1676).
In several ways Newcastle would be the likeliest candidate. In May 1638 he had
been appointed as governor of the eight-year-old Prince Charles. The prince's father,
Charles I, left London for York on 20 August 1640 and on 24 September convened
there a Great Council of Peers, which Newcastle probably attended." He and his
younger brother, Sir Charles Cavendish, the mathematician, to whom he was very
close, were noted scholars. Gascoigne was certainly known to the latter. Writing to
