Sir Kenelm Digby and His Alchemical
Circle in 1650s Paris: Newly Discovered
Manuscripts
Lawrence M. Principe
Johns Hopkins University, Baltimore, USA
A significant cache of hitherto unidentified manuscripts of Sir Kenelm Digby
(1603–1665) has been discovered in the Bibliothèque Nationale et Universi-
taire de Strasbourg. These manuscripts comprise over 5000 pages of material.
The documents relate almost entirely to metallic transmutation. About half of
the material consists of alchemical treatises (most of them unpublished) by
earlier authors — many relate to the transmuter Noël Picard, known as
Dubois, executed in 1637 — and show Digby ’s careful comparison of variant
readings in order to obtain the best text. The other half contains transcripts
from the otherwise lost notebooks of Joseph Du Chesne, Samuel Cottereau
Duclos (including several letters from Johann Rudolf Glauber), and others,
as well as reports of experiments and processes carried out by a range of infor-
mants and by Digby himself. Significantly, these manuscripts bear witness to
an important alchemical circle — of which Digby was a part — active in Paris
during the 1650s and 1660s. The members of this circle traded manuscripts
and information, and collaborated on a variety of alchemical projects;
several members were also involved in other, better-known, scientific group-
ings of mid-century Paris.
Introduction
Sir Kenelm Digby (1603–1665) ranks as one of the most colourful and engaging char-
acters of the seventeenth century. Widely travelled across Europe as a diplomat, courtier ,
scholar , and exile (not to mention his voyage to Turkey as a privateer), Digby interacted
with a broad array of important figures of his day . As an intellectual and author , his
activity encompassed natural philosophy, medicine, theology, literature, poetry , and
law. Given the diversity and intensity of his activities, it can be said that while Digby
has been the subject of several excellent studies, a rigorous and integrated portrait of

from our previously limited understanding of alchemy as a key part of seventeenth-
century experimental natural philosophy. Given the substantial progress made in the
past thirty years in terms of our knowledge of alchemy, the time is ripe for a fuller ,
more coherent, and better contextualised exposition of Digby ’s natural philosophy
and its influence.

Instead, I here wish only to report a discovery that should help in achieving that
greater goal, namely, the recovery of a significant collection of hitherto-unknown manu-
scripts of Sir Kenelm Digby entirely relating to alchemy. These manuscripts not only
reveal much more than has hitherto been known of Digby ’s interests and activities,
but also document a circle of his collaborators active in Paris during the 1650s and
1660s.
The documents in question are preserved among the outstanding collection of
manuscripts held by the Bibliothèque Nationale et Universitaire de Strasbourg.
Their identification as Digby ’s was partly a matter of good fortune. In summer
2010, I visited Strasbourg for the purpose of consulting two manuscripts— described
as eighteenth-century in the early twentieth-century catalogue of the collection —
that I hoped would shed new light on an elusive French chymist of the seventeenth
century. When the documents were delivered, I was astonished to find one of them
magnificently bound in fine leather , bearing a gilt fleur-de-lis on the cover and the
interlaced letters KD repeated in gold on the spine, instantly recognisable as the
monogram of Kenelm Digby which I had seen on another of his chymical manuscripts
uncovered at the Royal Society of London some years ago.

Dating and provenance
Since the primary language of the Strasbourg Digby manuscripts is French, and they
mention frequent personal contact with a host of Parisian chymists, they must date
from one or more of Digby ’s residences in the French capital. Digby ’s multiple
extended residences in Paris are spread over many years, from 1635 until 1660.
All of these manuscripts, however , almost certainly date from one or both of his
last two residences there in the 1650s — from around the start of the decade
through to the end of 1653, and from late 1655 until mid-1658.
