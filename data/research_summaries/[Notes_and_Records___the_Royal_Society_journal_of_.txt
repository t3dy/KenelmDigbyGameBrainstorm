KENELM DIGBY (17) 
O N the IIth of June 1603, three months after the death of Queen 
Elizabeth and four prior to that of her personal physician, the scientist 
William Gilbert, Kenelm Digby was born at Gayhurst, the family seat in 
Buckinghamshire. Digby at heart was an Elizabethan romantic who took 
all knowledge for his province-uomo universale. Of the Founder Members 
of the Royal Society none could point to so many diverse achievements in 
spheres other than science. Precocious as a schoolboy, with a flair for 
languages, Digby early in life earned fame as diplomat, privateer, courtier, 
poet, critic, stylist, philosopher, collector and binder of elegant books, and 
scientist of some attainment. 
I. FOREBEARS AND EARLY LIFE 
The family genealogist, Lettice Digby (I5) records that the Digby pedigree 
has been traced for 17 generations prior to James I and includes in each century 
prominent warriors and statesmen, nearly all of whom were nonconformists, 
often at odds with the society in which they moved. Kenelm was no 
exception. He belonged to a scholarly branch of the family, some of whom 
had been persecuted during the reign of Queen Elizabeth for embracing 
Catholicism as converts. His grandfather Everard Digby, philosopher and 
cleric of StJohn's College, Cambridge (Commoner 1567, Fellow 1573, M.A. 
1574), was the author of a book that suggested a systematic classification of 
the sciences (De duplice methodo, 1580) and also a treatise on swimming
