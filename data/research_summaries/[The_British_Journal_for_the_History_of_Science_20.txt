 Romancing the salve: Sir Kenelm Digby and 
 the powder of sympathy 
 ELIZABETH HEDRICK* 
 Abstract. Sir Kenelm Digby's A Late Discourse ... Touching the Cure of Wounds by the
 Powder of Sympathy (1658) is usually read in the context of seventeenth-century explana-
 tions of the weapon-salve. The salve supposedly worked by being applied to the weapon that
 made a wound rather than to the wound itself. But Digby's essay was as much an effort to
 claim priority for a powdered version of the sympathetic cure as an explanation of how the
 cure worked. A close examination of Digby's claims in the Late Discourse in the context of his
 own earlier work and of works by his contemporaries shows his priority claim to have been
 false. It was recognized as such by his most knowledgeable associates. The story of Digby's
 fabrications offers a case study of the generic and rhetorical terms in which seventeenth-
 century English thinkers made and challenged natural-philosophical claims. 
 In recent decades scholarly studies of the weapon-salve have illustrated the relevance of
 this early modern sympathetic cure to a number of larger subjects. The salve was sup-
 posedly Paracelsian in origin and was to be applied to the weapon that made a wound
 rather than to the wound itself. Walter Pagel and Alien Debus have detailed the extent
 to which it remained an object of intense interest into the seventeenth century.' Pagel, 
 Debus and others have also shown how this interest attests to the durability of the
 Paracelsian legacy and how the salve itself served as the locus of heated debates about
 the nature and theological legitimacy of actions at a distance in the early seventeenth
 century.2 Focusing on Sir Kenelm Digby's contribution to the literature on the weapon-

 (1658), Betty Jo Dobbs has noted that Digby's atomistic account of the cure's alleged
 operation serves as a useful example of the way in which natural philosophers before
 Newton were able to explain occult phenomena in more or less mechanistic terms.3
 As Dobbs explains, Digby's account of the powder's operation asserted its efficacy but
 'successfully eliminated the astral and spiritual influences' usually invoked in other 
 explanations of the sympathetic cure.4 
 Students of the cure often note that the primary difference between Digby's 
 powder and the weapon-salve is that the powder was a simple, dry compound - vitriol,
 dissolved in water for use - while the weapon-salve began as and remained a wet
 preparation consisting of various exotic ingredients such as bear grease, boar fat, blood,
 wine, skull moss and mummy (also called 'usnea').5 Because the powder and the salve 
 were used in a similar manner and posed similar explanatory challenges most scholars 
 have regarded them as interchangeable from a historical point of view. The essay that
 follows does not deny the utility of this view. However, it argues that scholarly 
 conflation of the two cures has obscured the meaningful question of why, in the Late
 Discourse, Digby expended so much effort to distinguish his 'powder' from the 'salve'
 with which so many of his contemporaries were already familiar. A re-examination of
 Digby's celebrated essay in fact shows that this work, reprinted forty times between
 1658 and the early eighteenth century, a text that continues to secure Digby's place in 
 discussions of the weapon-salve,6 was as much an attempt to establish the 'powder'
 itself as his intellectual property as it was an attempt to explain and legitimize the
 principles governing sympathetic cures. An examination of Digby's claim to priority 
 in the context of earlier writings both on the salve and on the powder, including 
 Digby's own, shows his claim to have been false. Moreover, a number of Digby's 
 contemporaries almost certainly knew that the stories he told to validate his ownership
 of the powder were fabrications. If historians have recurrently accepted Digby's tale
 without question - even some of the most authoritative of recent Digby scholars have
