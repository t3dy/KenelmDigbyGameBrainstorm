HOUGH Kenelm Henry Digby, a romantic convert, is a minor 
figure within the story of the development of nineteenth-century 
English Catholicism, his name is recalled in passing in many of the text 
books; and so, perhaps, some seventy years after Bernard Holland's 
sketchy biography of him, there is room for a reassessment of his place 
within Victorian Catholicism, in which milieu his name was well-known, 
his books widely read, and his person much-loved.1 In W. G. Roe's 
estimate, his writings 'made a considerable contribution, if not to the 
thought, at least to the atmosphere of the Catholic revival. '2 He was 
interesting as the first man to use the widespread fascination with 
the Middle Ages for the purpose of Catholic apologetic. When so 
distinguished a figure as Lord Acton noted his influence, 3 and a 
contributor to the Dublin Review suggested in 1843 that Digby's writings 
had helped to reduce anti-Catholic prejudice, 4 it is of interest to 
reconstruct his views on, and his contribution to, English Catholicism. 5 
The task is difficult, for he was and remains an elusive figure, somewhat 
isolated, uncontroversial, obsessively restless and given to writing 
numerous volumes of poor prose and terrible meditative poetry, which, 
despite their autobiographical nature, are frustratingly unrevealing. 
Born in Dublin, probably in 1796, 6 of an aristocratic, clerical, Church 
of Ireland family, he was brought up as a staunch anti-Catholic, with 
lasting interests in religion, travel, literature and history, interests which 
combined to lead to his conversion to Catholicism. His parents died 
during his childhood, and he was sent to school in London, whence he 
passed to Trinity College, Cambridge in 1815. While still at school he 
chanced to meet two prominent Catholic laymen, the Cisalpines Charles 
Butler and Sir Henry Englefield, who made a lasting impression on 
him. He seldom returned to his native country, but early travels on the 
Continent exposed him to Catholic culture, while his historical interest 
alerted him to the Catholics in his own family's past. 
At Cambridge he met Charles Shore (later Lord Teignmouth), who 
judged him to be 'a man of rare spirit and acquirements', whose 'soul 
and spirit were imbued with medieval chivalry.' 7 Graduating in 1819, 
with his tutor William Whewell' s help he continued to live at Trinity. 
Both Whewell and the clergyman tutor Julius Charles Hare were his 
long-term friends, and both were unsympathetic to anti-Catholic big­
otry, in that respect doubtless softening his own prejudice. Hare's 
interest in continental theology led him to urge Digby to read German

In the earlier 1820s he was an 'Anglo-Catholic', but converted to 
Rome in 1825. In April 1825 the minor poet William Sidney Walker 
noted that Digby had 'become as good as Catholic', and lamented the 
fact in a verse addressed to Rome: 
This nobler soul, for thee too rich a prize; 
High-minded, gentle-hearted; innocent 
As childhood, yet on manliest thoughts intent; 
Lover of truth, by specious sanctities,

To error ... 8 
In later years Digby reconstructed why in the 1820s he had found 
Catholicism so attractive: he had felt the need, as he put it, for Catholic 
ideology, ritual and custom, for its love of humanity, its 'deep respect' 
for 'the most despised, reviled,. and blamed', and valued its 'great aim 
to make us one', which last was to be always a deep concern with him, 
as for many romantic medievalists. Above all, he felt Catholicism 
helped him with his fear of death. 9 Later in the year 1825 he went to 
Charles Butler for advice about becoming a Catholic. Butler sent him 
to a Jesuit, who gave him instructions and then received him.10 After 
his conversion he came to know well the Cisalpine recusant family of 
the Huddlestons, the head of which lived at Sawston Hall, outside 
Cambridge. 
In 1826 Digby was introduced to another recent convert, Ambrose 
Phillipps de Lisle, a new member of Trinity, and took the role of 
mentor to the younger man, who was already well-read, and shared his 
own passion for medieval literature. Bishop William Poynter, the Vicar 
Apostolic of the London District, told de Lisle, 'I am glad that you 
have such an excellent companion . . . as Mr. Digby', and advised 
him that he could not cultivate 'a more valuable correspondence than 
that which you hold with him.' 11 De Lisle repeatedly expressed his debt 
to Digby, and so it is likely that Digby influenced him in the way of 
medievalism, and possibly in a certain eirenic spirit which softened 
Digby's own ultramontanism, itself derived from reading Count Joseph 
de Maistre and Count Louis de Bonald. De Lisle went on to be 
an unyielding advocate of medievalism in worship and culture-the 
Cistercian abbey of Mount St. Bernard, founded ip. 1835, being built 
on his land-and a prominent proponent of ecumenical relations with 
Anglicanism. Part of the inspiration for these things probably issued

Digby was a monarchist and conservative, even tending as a young 
man to favour monarchic-cum-aristocratic rule, provided that the mon­
arch and his co.,.rulers were good Catholics. He developed this view in 
his paradoxically anarchic writings, which had a certain influence in 
their day, not only on ultramontanists, like Faber, but on Young 
England Conservatism.13 In 1822 he published The Broad Stone of 
Honour, which proposed that society be ruled by an aristocracy of 
Christian gentlemen, whose privileged status would be a function of 
the conscientious execution of their paternalist duties towards the lower 
classes. He romantically hoped that the existing aristocracy could be 
educated to match this ideal. An expanded, more Catholic, second 
edition appeared the following year, and its prologue stated his ambition 
in writing it, which was ·to christianize the English aristocracy. 14 Fol­
lowing his conversion, he judged these editions to be insufficiently 
Catholic, and re-wrote the work in four volumes, published from 1826 
onwards, 15 with subsequent editions. In these he presented a picture of 
medieval Catholicism which, though highly romanticized, was neverthe­
less based on his comprehensive reading in medieval literature. Although 
his writing was as diffuse and prolix as his reading was prodigious-he 
must have been one of the most well-read men of nineteenth-century 
Britain-his book was well calculated to charm romantic spirits to an 
appreciation of Catholic heritage. The words 'duty' and 'gentleman' 
became key words of the Victorian period, and the Broad Stone (the 
second edition of which was subtitled Rules for the Gentlemen of 
England) certainly had some role in making that so.16 
It is plain from the Broad Stone that Digby was searching for 
religious, social and political order, which he saw fulfilled in hierarchy, 
with the Pope at the top of the pyramid. Moreover, if the socio-political 
order was not based on Christianity it was doomed to failure. His 
anxiety about disorder and his quest for unity were not surprising in 
one brought up during the Napoleonic Wars, in a society still frightened 
by the memory of the French Revolution. Tradition was the answer to 
revolution, and since that was to be found in monarchism and papalism 
he embraced the 'Throne and Altar' ideology cultivated by de Maistre 
and de Bonald, who taught that an infallible papacy was the keystone 
of authoritarian, monarchical rule, and that, conversely, monarchy was 
the best means of propagating the papacy. 
This politico-religious vision was not far removed from that of 
Frederick William Faber, whose book Sights and Thoughts in Foreign 
Churches and Among Foreign Peoples (1842) is reminiscent of the 
Broad Stone: indeed, the phrase 'the broad stone of honour' occurs in 
the book. 17 Faber later came to know Digby personally .18 It may be,
